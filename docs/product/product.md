# SampleByte

## What it is

SampleByte is a source-agnostic sample preparation and pack-building workstation for producers who turn their own audio into playable sample packs. It covers the workflow from import or recording through chopping, refining, organizing, and exporting target-ready folders for hardware samplers, pad controllers, DAWs, and other music production setups.

## Audience

The primary user is a producer who already has sounds from vinyl, synths, field recordings, phone recordings, DAW bounces, Freesound, old folders, or other sources and needs to prepare them for use. The early adopter is a hardware or software producer who repeatedly turns their own sounds into playable packs: SP-404, MPC, Maschine, keyboard, and pad-controller users, plus DAW users who want organized sample folders.

They care because the current workflow is spread across too many tools: Finder or Explorer, Audacity or Ableton, sample sites, manual chopping, manual naming, format conversion, and copying files to the target device or folder.

## Positioning

SampleByte should position as a source-agnostic sample preparation and pack-building workstation, not as a sample marketplace, mini-DAW, sequencer, effects processor, or single-device utility. The differentiator is the fast flow from import or record, to chop and refine, to organize and find, to assemble and export target-ready packs. Freesound is an optional source input, not the core promise.

The durable edge is target export profiles, pack/project workflow, and a local sample history and library that make repeated preparation faster over time.

## Direction Assessment

Verdict: conditional go. The direction is strong for a focused owner-user workflow and likely useful for producers who prepare packs repeatedly.

The conditions are to keep scope narrow around sample preparation and export, remove ambiguity around save-to-library behavior, and validate that users outside the creator's own workflow experience the pain often enough to change habits. SampleByte should avoid competing directly with DAWs, sample marketplaces, sequencers, or broad effects environments.

## Key Risks

- The segment may be small or may tolerate manual workflows. Test this by finding 10 producers who prepare packs repeatedly and observing how often the pain recurs.
- Save, library, project, and pack semantics can confuse first-time users. Test the first-run flow without explanation and watch whether users understand what is live, saved, reusable, and exported.
- Scope creep into a DAW, marketplace, or effects product would dilute the wedge. Keep roadmap decisions anchored to sample preparation and target-ready export.
